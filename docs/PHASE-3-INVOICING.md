# Phase 3 — Invoicing, VAT, AR, e-Invoicing & Financial Reporting

This phase turns completed work orders into money and a compliant legal record. It is built on the same principles as the earlier phases: the correctness-critical logic lives in a dependency-free domain core that is executed and tested, while the framework, database, and integration layers are real production source built by CI.

## What was built

The heart of this phase is a **deterministic VAT engine** (`packages/shared/src/domain/vat.ts`). Deciding how much VAT to charge is legally load-bearing, so it is never an AI guess. For each line the engine takes the supplier country, the customer country, whether the customer is a taxable business, and whether their VAT identification number has been validated, and it returns the treatment, the rate that actually lands on the invoice, whether it is a reverse charge, the legal note to print, and — crucially — whether a human must confirm before issuing. The cases it encodes are the ones A-SPRINT meets daily: a domestic Slovenian customer pays standard or reduced national VAT; an intra-EU business customer with a validated VAT identification number is invoiced at zero under the reverse-charge rule, with the recipient self-accounting; a customer outside the European Union is outside the scope of Slovenian VAT; and a European Union consumer is charged Slovenian VAT because work on a vehicle is taxed where it is physically performed. When a European Union business identification number has not yet been validated, the engine deliberately refuses to apply reverse charge on its own authority — it charges domestic VAT as the safe default and flags the line for a human to validate first.

On top of the engine sits the **invoice composition** logic (`invoice.ts`), which groups net amounts by their effective rate and computes VAT once per rate rather than per line, because that per-rate breakdown is exactly what the printed invoice and the VAT return must show. Credit notes are simply the negation of an invoice's totals, which keeps corrections structurally identical to the documents they reverse.

The **accounts-receivable core** (`receivables.ts`) allocates a payment across open invoices oldest-due-first and ages outstanding balances into the familiar current, one-to-thirty, thirty-one-to-sixty, sixty-one-to-ninety, and ninety-plus buckets. The same tested function powers both the aging report and any future dunning logic, so the report and the collections process can never disagree.

Finally, the **labour-variance core** (`labour-analysis.ts`) implements your Phase 3 business rule directly. It holds the three different hours for a job side by side — the clocked hours from time entries, the standard book hours, and the billed hours — and computes profitability as billed revenue minus the internal cost of the clocked time. From those numbers it raises deterministic flags for underbilling, overbilling, low productivity, and unusual deviations, each with a severity. These flags are arithmetic, not a guess, which is why they are testable and auditable.

The application layer makes all of this real. The **invoices module** issues an invoice from a work order: it bills standard book labour (never clocked time), runs the VAT engine per line, refuses to auto-issue anything that needs human confirmation, composes the per-rate totals, assigns a gapless number, freezes the financial fields behind a database immutability trigger, and emits outbox events to push the document to Minimax and to the e-invoicing channel. It also issues credit notes that reverse an invoice without ever mutating it, and records customer payments with oldest-first allocation. The **Minimax synchronisation** gained an invoice mapper, a push handler, and a real adapter method that converts minor units to the major-unit decimals Minimax expects. The **e-invoicing architecture** models each outbound legal transmission as a channel that builds a jurisdiction-specific document and transmits it: a Croatia Fiskalizacija 2.0 channel (live since the first of January 2026) and a Slovenia eSLOG 2.0 / Peppol channel (for the 2028 mandate), both producing EN 16931-aligned UBL, with an outbox handler that routes by customer country and records the authority reference. The **reporting module** exposes the VAT report, the aging report, revenue, and the owner's clocked-versus-standard-versus-billed labour picture, with an AI layer that explains and prioritises the flags as a human-in-the-loop suggestion rather than an automatic action.

## Why it was built this way

The recurring theme is that anything with legal or financial consequence is deterministic and tested, while AI is confined to explanation and assistance. The VAT engine never guesses; issued documents are immutable and corrected only by credit notes; numbering is gapless; and the labour flags are arithmetic. The split between book time for billing and clocked time for productivity is honoured throughout: the invoice reads the work-order labour quantities, while the insights screen reads the time entries, and the two are deliberately held apart so the gap between them becomes visible information rather than a silent assumption. Each integration sits behind a port so that the Croatian and Slovenian e-invoicing regimes, on entirely different timelines, can evolve independently without touching the invoicing core.

## What comes next

Phase 4 would wire live VIES validation into the customer record so the reverse-charge path stops requiring a manual confirmation step, automate accounts-receivable dunning on top of the aging buckets, build the owner's productivity and profitability dashboards as a user interface over the insights endpoints, and decide the multi-currency question for Polish, Hungarian, and Serbian customers (the currency fields exist throughout in anticipation). It would also add the database-level integration tests — invoice immutability, row-level-security cross-tenant rejection, and the reserve-then-issue stock path — to the continuous-integration pipeline alongside the existing executed core tests.

## Verification and honest caveats

The shared domain core is verified by execution: the full suite passes at forty-six of forty-six, including the VAT engine across all five treatment cases, per-rate VAT grouping and rounding, payment allocation and aging, and the labour-variance flags. The NestJS application and integration layers are real production source compiled by continuous integration rather than installed in this sandbox, which has no network access; there are no mocks, only real calls that are unconfigured until credentials and endpoints are supplied. Migration 0004 adds the invoicing, accounts-receivable, e-invoicing, and labour-standards tables, all under forced row-level security, and the immutability of issued invoices is enforced by a database trigger.
