# Customer Portal — Milestone Report

## What was built

The Customer Portal is a complete, separately-authenticated surface that lets a
workshop's customers see and act on their own work without staff involvement,
built entirely on the existing backend rather than a parallel data store. It has
two halves. On the server, a portal module exposes a small, deliberately narrow
API: a public pair of authentication endpoints and, behind a portal session,
read access to the customer's own profile, vehicles, open and historical work
orders, invoices, downloadable invoice PDFs, a document archive, pending
approvals, and appointment requests — plus the two write actions a customer is
allowed to take, namely responding to an approval and requesting an appointment.
On the client, a mobile-first set of screens under `/portal` covers the magic-link
landing, a home dashboard, the work-order list and a status-tracking detail, an
approvals screen, an invoice list and detail with a PDF download, a service
history, and an appointment-request form, all sharing a phone-first UI kit with a
bottom navigation bar.

Authentication is passwordless by design. A customer reaches the portal through a
link in an SMS or email — the deep-link mechanism the communication architecture
called for — carrying a short-lived signed token that proves which customer in
which tenant is asking. The server verifies that token and mints a portal session
stored only as a hash, so even a database leak does not reveal usable session
tokens. The signing and verification live in the shared core as
`PortalToken.createPortalToken`/`verifyPortalToken`, using HMAC-SHA256 with a
constant-time comparison, expiry and purpose checks, and a refusal to operate with
a weak secret; they are covered by seven dedicated tests.

## Why it was built this way

Two decisions shaped everything. The first is the passwordless magic-link model,
chosen because the customers are hauliers who will not create or remember yet
another password, and because the workshop already needs to send them SMS and
email anyway — so the same message that says "your truck is ready" can carry the
link that proves who they are. This also keeps the portal free of any password
storage, which is one less thing to secure. The second is defence in depth on
isolation. A customer portal has a stricter requirement than the staff app: it is
not enough to keep tenant A's data away from tenant B; customer X must never see
customer Y's data within the same tenant. So every portal query enforces both
layers — the tenant's row-level security as always, and an explicit customer_id
predicate bound from the verified session — and the scope guard refuses any
request that does not carry a customer identity, which means a staff token cannot
reach portal data even by accident. Child rows such as work-order lines and
invoice lines are fetched only after their parent has been proven to belong to the
customer, so they inherit that isolation rather than re-deriving it. Every
state-changing action a customer takes is written to the same hash-chain audit the
rest of the system uses, so an approval or an appointment request is as traceable
as any staff action.

The invoice PDF is generated by a dependency-free generator rather than a library,
consistent with the project's constraint of avoiding heavy dependencies, and it
produces a clean, professional document with the workshop letterhead, the
customer's bill-to details, the line items, the totals, and the correct VAT
treatment note — including the reverse-charge wording for cross-border EU
customers, which matters for exactly the Croatian hauliers this workshop serves.

## Verification results

Everything verifiable in an offline environment passes. The shared core is green
at 104 of 104 tests, including the seven portal-token tests. All 119 backend files
parse cleanly, including the whole portal module and the notifications port. On the
frontend, every portal screen's @/ imports resolve, no screen imports a library
that is not a declared dependency, and — checked specifically — every portalApi
method the screens call is defined on the client and maps to a real route the
portal controller serves, with the public/authed split correct (only the
request-link and verify routes are public). The customer-isolation audit confirmed
that every directly-queried customer entity carries an explicit customer_id filter
and that the three statements without one are correct by construction (the profile
row keyed by its own id, and child line-item rows constrained by an already-scoped
parent). The invoice PDF generator was executed against its true data contract and
produced a valid single-page %PDF-1.4 of 2,130 bytes that parses as one page and
renders to a clean invoice showing the number, customer, line items, totals and
reverse-charge note. The portal also runs in the approved demo mode, auto-signing
in as a demo Croatian customer so the portal can be shown from the same Viber link
as the staff app.

## Remaining risks

The standing environment caveat applies unchanged: the pure shared logic and the
PDF generator are proven by execution, but the NestJS portal module and the React
screens are real production source verified by parse-check and import/route
cross-checks, not by a live database, server, or browser. The genuine final gate is
running the portal against a deployed instance with migration 0010 applied.

Three things are deliberately not yet production-complete. The notification
provider is a stub adapter that logs the message rather than sending it, so in the
current state the magic link is recoverable from the server log in development but
no real SMS or email is dispatched; wiring the real provider (Infobip, behind the
existing port) is the next step before a real customer could log in unaided. VAT-id
validation for cross-border customers still falls back to manual confirmation when
the VIES service is unavailable. And the document archive currently surfaces
invoices as PDFs; other document types (e.g. the work-order "nalog" as a customer
copy) are a natural extension but not yet exposed.

## Customer Portal readiness assessment

Against the success criteria, the portal meets them at the level this environment
can prove. A customer can log in (magic-link verification mints a session), see
their vehicles, see work-order progress with a friendly status and progress
indication, approve or decline additional work and estimates, view their invoices
with a payment status, download a valid invoice PDF, and track their service
history — and each of those paths is wired screen-to-client-to-route with the
isolation and audit guarantees intact. The portal is therefore functionally
complete and architecturally sound for a pilot, with one true blocker before an
unaccompanied real-world login: connecting the live notification provider so the
magic link actually reaches the customer's phone. Everything else on the list is
either done or a clearly-scoped enhancement rather than a gap in the core.

No OCR, plate-recognition, or voice-work-order work was begun.
