# Final Presentation Guide — Workshop OS

*A guide for presenting the demo to A-SPRINT or to a commercial audience. It offers
a narrative order to walk, the points to emphasise, and honest framing for the
parts that are demonstrations rather than live integrations.*

---

## 1. The story to tell

Workshop OS is not a collection of screens; it is one connected system where a
customer connects to a vehicle, a vehicle to a work order, a work order to inventory
and to an invoice, and an invoice to accounting. The most effective presentation
follows that spine rather than touring features at random, because the spine is the
product's actual argument: everything is linked, and the AI assists at each step
without ever taking an irreversible action on its own.

Open by handing the audience the link (or a QR code) and letting them open it on
their own phones while you present. That single act — they are inside the product in
one tap, no setup — makes the core promise before you say a word.

## 2. A suggested walkthrough order

Begin at the **launcher**, pointing out that one identity reaches every role:
mechanic, advisor, owner, and the worker's own time clock. This frames the breadth.

Move to the **Service Advisor** desk and create the spine: look up a customer, open
their vehicle, start a **work order**, add a labour line and a part. This is the
everyday core; keep it quick to show how few taps it takes.

Show the **AI inputs** that reduce typing, which is the founding promise of the
product. Scan a **plate** to pull up a vehicle; dictate a **voice work order**; and
in the warehouse, photograph a **delivery note** for **OCR** receiving. Emphasise
the airlock each time: the AI proposes, a human confirms — nothing is written
without approval. This is the right moment to say that the AI is advisory by design.

Visit the **warehouse** to show inventory, stock movements, and receiving as the
supply side of the same work orders.

Go to **invoicing**: issue the invoice from the work order, show the VAT breakdown
(including reverse-charge for cross-border EU hauliers, which matters for A-SPRINT's
customer base), and then let the **Minimax sync panel** complete on screen — this is
the accounting integration, shown end to end.

Open the **Owner dashboard** and the **AI Workshop Manager** to show the payoff: the
system reads its own data and surfaces real issues — an overdue receivable, a
loss-making job, low stock — prioritised and explained, with every number traceable
to a record. Stress that the manager only advises; it changes nothing.

Show **Vehicle Rental** as a complete second business line: reserve a vehicle, hand
it over, return it, watch the charges compute, and issue the rental invoice through
the very same engine.

Close with the **Customer Portal** so the audience sees the customer's own view —
history, approvals, invoices — and then use the **Share card** to send the demo to
someone in the room, demonstrating the hand-off live.

## 3. What to emphasise

Three themes land best: that everything is one connected system rather than separate
tools; that the AI removes typing and clicking but never acts irreversibly on its
own; and that the documents and accounting are real and Slovenian-compliant
(VAT, reverse charge, Minimax, gapless numbering). These are the points a workshop
owner and an accountant care about.

## 4. Honest framing for demonstrations

Present these accurately, because credibility is worth more than polish. The AI
features run on a built-in sample engine in the demo, so their output is
representative rather than live — say so, and note that connecting the real
EU-resident model is configuration, not redevelopment. The Minimax panel is a
faithful demonstration of the real sync flow, not a push to a live Minimax
organisation. And the demo holds no real data, which is exactly why it is safe to
share freely. None of this diminishes the demo; stated plainly, it builds trust.

## 5. Transition to the pilot

End the commercial conversation by connecting the demo to the path forward: the same
system, deployed to staging and then to a supervised A-SPRINT pilot, with a human
keeping a hand on invoicing and Minimax until each proves itself. The demo shows the
destination; the Pilot Execution Guide shows the road. That framing turns a
presentation into a credible next step rather than a one-off impression.

## 6. Pre-presentation checklist

Before presenting: confirm the public URL is clean and opens to the launcher on a
fresh phone; do the real-device pass (Mobile Readiness Report, Section 5) on an
Android and an iPhone; have the QR code ready; pick a frozen tagged deployment so a
redeploy cannot change what the audience sees mid-talk; and walk the Section 2 order
once yourself so the spine flows.
